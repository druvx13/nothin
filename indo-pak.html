Historical Context and Escalation of Tensions

The Wagah border crossing between India and Pakistan, a symbol of their complex relationship (Photo: Unsplash)

The historical trajectory of India-Pakistan relations has been marked by persistent volatility, with recurring cycles of conflict rooted in territorial disputes, particularly over Kashmir. Since their partition in 1947, the two nuclear-armed neighbors have engaged in multiple wars and skirmishes, each exacerbating the underlying mistrust and animosity.

Post-2020, this fraught relationship witnessed several critical flashpoints that set the stage for the dramatic escalation in 2025. Among these, the Pahalgam attack on April 22, 2025, emerged as a pivotal event, triggering a chain reaction of retaliatory measures and international diplomatic interventions.

"The world cannot afford a military confrontation between India and Pakistan."

— UN Secretary-General António Guterres, May 6, 2025

The Pahalgam Attack: Catalyst for Crisis

On April 22, 2025, a terrorist attack in Pahalgam, Indian-administered Kashmir, claimed the lives of 26 civilians, including 25 Hindu tourists, a Christian tourist, and a local Muslim resident. The Resistance Front (TRF), an insurgent group allegedly linked to the Pakistan-based Lashkar-e-Taiba (LeT), initially claimed responsibility but later retracted its statement.

This ambiguity fueled India's accusations against Pakistan, asserting that Islamabad was complicit in sponsoring cross-border terrorism—a charge vehemently denied by Pakistan. The attack not only deepened the humanitarian crisis in the region but also reignited longstanding grievances between the two nations.

Pahalgam, a popular tourist destination in Kashmir, became the site of the attack that triggered the 2025 crisis (Photo: Unsplash)

India's Retaliatory Measures

Suspension of Indus Waters Treaty

India suspended the 1960 agreement governing river resource sharing, prompting Pakistan to warn this would be considered an "Act of War".

Diplomatic Expulsions

India revoked visas for Pakistani diplomats and expelled several officials, significantly reducing diplomatic channels for communication.

Airspace Restrictions

India closed 27 airports near the border and imposed airspace restrictions, disrupting civilian flights and trade routes.

International Interventions and Diplomatic Efforts

The United Nations played a crucial role in mediating the crisis (Photo: Unsplash)

The year 2025 marked a significant escalation in tensions between India and Pakistan, prompting an array of international interventions and diplomatic efforts aimed at averting a potential catastrophe. At the forefront of these efforts was UN Secretary-General António Guterres, whose repeated appeals for restraint underscored the gravity of the situation.

His statements, issued on May 6 and reinforced through subsequent briefings, highlighted the catastrophic implications of a military confrontation between two nuclear-armed states. By emphasizing that "the world cannot afford a military confrontation," Guterres not only drew global attention to the crisis but also reiterated the indispensable role of multilateral institutions like the United Nations in mediating high-stakes conflicts.

UN Security Council Consultations

On May 5, 2025, the UN Security Council convened closed consultations under the agenda item "The India-Pakistan Question," marking the first formal discussion since the crisis began. During these deliberations, Assistant Secretary-General Mohamed Khaled Khiari briefed members on behalf of the Department of Political and Peacebuilding Affairs (DPPA) and the Department of Peace Operations (DPO).

While Council members unanimously condemned the April 22 terror attack in Pahalgam, which resulted in 26 civilian deaths, disagreements arose over references to the Resistance Front (TRF) and the labeling of Jammu and Kashmir as a "disputed territory". Pakistan, supported by China, sought to include language describing the region as disputed, a proposal met with resistance from other members.

Ceasefire Negotiations

Conflicting reports emerged regarding the mediation of the May 10 ceasefire. While President Trump claimed sole responsibility for the agreement, Pakistani officials credited a broader coalition of nations, including Saudi Arabia, Iran, Turkey, Qatar, the UK, and China, for their contributions.

This discrepancy underscores the multifaceted nature of international mediation in South Asia, where no single actor holds exclusive influence. Furthermore, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio's mention of plans for broader talks at a neutral venue contradicted India's official stance, revealing underlying tensions in the diplomatic process.

Economic Sanctions and Trade Disruptions

The Wagah-Attari border crossing was closed during the crisis, disrupting formal trade (Photo: Unsplash)

The economic relationship between India and Pakistan has long been a complex interplay of geopolitical tensions, historical grievances, and mutual dependencies. However, recent escalations in hostilities have brought economic sanctions and trade disruptions to the forefront, amplifying diplomatic tensions while exposing vulnerabilities within both economies.

One of the most significant developments was India's decision to suspend the 1960 Indus Waters Treaty following the April 22, 2025, Pahalgam attack. The treaty, which allocates approximately 80% of the shared river flows to Pakistan, is critical for sustaining its agricultural sector—a backbone of the Pakistani economy.

The Indus Waters Treaty Crisis

By threatening to restrict water flow under the treaty, India effectively weaponized a vital natural resource, prompting warnings from Pakistan that such actions would constitute an "Act of War". This move underscores how resource-sharing agreements can become flashpoints in bilateral conflicts when linked to accusations of terrorism or other provocations.

For Pakistan, the suspension poses existential risks to its agriculture-dependent communities, particularly those reliant on irrigation systems fed by the Indus River system. Experts argue that these measures could deepen mistrust, making future negotiations more contentious and heightening the risk of military escalation over shared resources.

Military Engagements and Skirmishes

Advanced military hardware was deployed by both sides during the conflict (Photo: Unsplash)

The year 2025 marked a significant escalation in hostilities between India and Pakistan, with military engagements along the Line of Control (LoC) reaching unprecedented levels of intensity. The conflict began on April 23, 2025, following a terrorist attack in Pahalgam, Jammu and Kashmir, which claimed the lives of 27 individuals.

This incident triggered a series of retaliatory actions by India, including the suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty, visa cancellations, expulsion of diplomats, and closure of airspace and borders. These measures set the stage for cross-border skirmishes that would define the ensuing weeks.

Key Military Operations

Operation Sindoor (India)

Date: May 7, 2025

Targets: Nine locations across Pakistani-administered Kashmir and Punjab province

Weapons Used: Rafale aircraft with SCALP missiles, BrahMos cruise missiles, Israeli Harop loitering munitions

Claimed Results: Approximately 100 militants killed, including high-profile figures like Abdul Rauf Azhar

Operation Bunyan al-Marsus (Pakistan)

Date: May 10, 2025

Targets: Multiple Indian airbases near Nur Khan, Shorkot, and Murid

Weapons Used: Drones, missiles

Claimed Results: Significant damage to Indian airbases, marking first use of drones in combat between nuclear powers

Technological Milestones

First Drone Warfare Between Nuclear Powers

The use of drones in combat during these operations represented a historic milestone, marking the first instance of drone warfare between two nuclear-armed nations. This technological advancement underscored the evolving nature of modern warfare and heightened concerns about potential escalations.

Ceasefire and Aftermath

The ceasefire agreement announced on May 10, 2025, was brokered through intense diplomatic efforts led by the United States, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Turkey, Qatar, the UK, and China. Notably, U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Vice President JD Vance played pivotal roles in mediating the truce.

Despite initial violations reported by both parties, calm eventually prevailed, underscoring the critical role of international mediation in averting catastrophic outcomes. However, expert perspectives on the tactical gains versus strategic importance of these military engagements remain divided.

Conclusion and Path Forward

Symbol of peace and hope for future reconciliation (Photo: Unsplash)

The 2025 India-Pakistan crisis serves as a stark reminder of the complex interplay between historical grievances, geopolitical maneuvering, and military brinkmanship that defines relations between the two nuclear-armed neighbors. The sequence of events, beginning with the Pahalgam attack on April 22, 2025, and culminating in the May 10 ceasefire, underscores the fragility of Indo-Pak relations and the potential for localized violence to escalate into broader conflict with severe regional and global implications.

While the ceasefire offers a temporary reprieve, unresolved issues such as the suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty, allegations of cross-border terrorism, and the contentious Kashmir dispute remain potent flashpoints that could reignite tensions.

Key Lessons and Recommendations

Strengthening Diplomatic Channels

Establish permanent backchannel communications to prevent misunderstandings and enable rapid de-escalation during crises.

Resource Sharing Agreements

Revitalize the Indus Waters Treaty with independent monitoring mechanisms to depoliticize water resource management.

People-to-People Contacts

Expand cultural exchanges, trade delegations, and academic collaborations to build grassroots support for peace.

Nuclear Risk Reduction

Implement bilateral agreements on no-first-use policies and advance notification of missile tests to enhance strategic stability.

Media Responsibility

Create joint media councils to combat misinformation and promote responsible reporting during tensions.

International Engagement

Leverage multilateral forums like SAARC and SCO for confidence-building measures while respecting bilateral nature of core disputes.

The international community's intervention, particularly through the United Nations and key global powers like the United States, Saudi Arabia, and China, played a crucial role in averting large-scale violence. However, the absence of a clear consensus on fundamental issues like Kashmir and water-sharing highlights the limitations of current diplomatic frameworks and underscores the need for innovative approaches to foster dialogue and build trust between India and Pakistan.

Future research should explore the intersection of environmental and geopolitical tensions, particularly regarding shared water resources, as these issues will likely become increasingly salient in the context of climate change. Such insights could inform more effective strategies for preventing future crises and promoting long-term peace in South Asia.
